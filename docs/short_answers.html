Question 3: This is a mid-career data scientist position. Please describe your interest in this position and how it fits into your current career goals.

My interest in this position is the product of a decade spent developing data science skills at a public research university, and a deep desire to utilize those skills in public service. Academic research has taught me to work rigorously with large, messy administrative data such as court records, cancer registries, and vital records, and throughout my tenure I’ve found most meaning in applied work that views social science as public problem-solving: research evidence applied equitably and ethically, alongside the communities it’s meant to serve. Evaluating the H-SYNC pilot program with UW’s CoLab for Community & Behavioral Health Policy, I saw analysis move directly into decisions about a program serving court-involved youth. As a community partner with King County’s Community Resilience + Equity Program, I worked with county staff to translate my research on air pollution and psychological distress toward participatory, community-engaged public-health practice. And as a Data Science for Social Good fellow, I built tools that showed regional transit planners which communities their system underserved — evidence to inform policy that treats transit as a collective good, one that should serve everyone equitably and efficiently. Experiences like these have been the high points of my research career, and as I transition out of formal academic training, I want them to be the foundation of what comes next. That is exactly what draws me to the PME unit’s mandate to turn data into better outcomes for King County residents.

BHRD’s focus makes this position more than a good technical fit. Mental and behavioral health has been a through-line of my research — from first-authored work on air pollution and psychological distress to a dissertation examining how a weight-centered health paradigm shapes behaviors and accompanying mental and physical health outcomes. That focus is not incidental. Mental health and substance use challenges have directly affected my family, friends, and loved ones and I have seen firsthand how much depends on whether care and support are available and accessible when needed. My research has deepened and contextualized that experience: because so many of the conditions that produce these challenges — poverty, housing instability, discrimination, environmental harm — are structural, public programs and policies are among the most powerful levers we have for improving individual and community health. Supporting the crisis continuum with careful, equity-centered analysis is work I would find deeply rewarding.

Finally, this role aligns with where I have most fun working with data and where I want to continue to grow. A consistent thread of my career has been translating complex analyses into insights people can easily understand and act upon: distilling survival models into visual summaries that let H-SYNC program staff and court partners see what the program was achieving; building an interactive dashboard that let transit planners explore millions of trip records intuitively and informatively; and designing visualizations that make complex research findings legible to expert and lay audiences alike. Five years of drop-in consulting at UW’s Center for Social Science Computation and Research — and teaching R to students with no programming background — have taught me to meet any audience at their level of technical comfort and bring them along. A position that asks me to pair advanced analysis with clear, actionable communication, in service of a community I care deeply about, is exactly what I have been looking for - work I want to keep doing and getting better at, for a long time to come.

Question 5: King County has transformed its work on equity and social justice from an initiative to an integrated effort that applies the principle of “fair and just” intentionally in all the county does in order to achieve equitable opportunities for all people and communities. Describe how you have placed principles related to equity and social justice at the center of your work and give specific examples if possible. If you do have this experience, please write “N/A”.

Equity and social justice are not simply a component of my work; they are the motivation and lens through which I orient my life. My training as a sociologist is, at its core, training in seeing how systems and structures produce and reproduce inequality — how outcomes that appear individual are actually patterned by place, race, policy, and history. Every research question I have pursued has grown from that understanding. My earliest published work documented how residential segregation concentrates air pollution — and the psychological distress it causes — in communities of color. In Belgium, working with individually linked census and cancer registry records, I measured how socioeconomic position, race, ethnicity, and nativity structure who develops cancer and who survives it, identifying where policy intervention could interrupt those inequities. My dissertation traces how a weight-centered health paradigm with racialized origins shapes health behaviors and outcomes differently by race, sex, class, and neighborhood context. Each of these projects asks, in its own way, where inequity enters and where it can be interrupted.

That orientation matters most precisely where data work looks purely technical, because data is never neutral. Administrative data records the behavior of systems as much as the people in them, and I treat that as an analytic fact with consequences. As a Data Science for Social Good fellow our team quantified who was missing from 21 million transit fare records — riders who pay cash, who are disproportionately low-income — and defined what the data could and could not support, so that a dashboard meant to advance equity would not quietly encode exclusion. In evaluating King County’s H-SYNC pilot, I estimated differential program impacts by race, ethnicity, sex, and geography, and interpreted results in light of how systemic inequities shape both the outcomes youth experience and the administrative records that document them.

The same principles govern my work outside of research. As a teacher, I moved to an ungraded model — built on iterative feedback and student self-assessment — because conventional grading reproduces existing inequities in background and preparation rather than measuring learning. As a community partner with King County’s Community Resilience + Equity Program, I worked with county staff to translate environmental-justice research into participatory, community-engaged practice. Centering equity and social justice is at the heart of my training and the work I’ve done throughout my career, and I am drawn to this position because it offers the chance to keep doing so within a team that has made that orientation its explicit framework.

Question 8: Describe your experience supporting program monitoring and evaluation processes (e.g., development of performance measures, designing an evaluation protocol, collecting data, monitoring program performance, and/or using data to support continuous quality improvement efforts). Please include the organization(s) and dates of employment and highlight evaluation of behavioral health or crisis system programs as relevant. If you do not have this experience, please type N/A.

My most direct evaluation experience comes from my current role with the CoLab for Community & Behavioral Health Policy in UW’s Department of Psychiatry and Behavioral Sciences (January 2024–present), where I partnered with the CoLab Director to design and execute a summative evaluation of the Housing Stability for Youth in Courts (H-SYNC) pilot, a continuum-of-care intervention for court-involved youth at risk of housing instability. I contributed to every stage of the evaluation: defining the evaluation questions, operationalizing outcomes from administrative records, constructing the analytic dataset by extracting and matching individual-level records across two large administrative databases and program service data, building a matched comparison group optimized for covariate balance across 21 demographic and risk metrics, estimating program effects on recidivism through survival analysis, and examining differential impacts by race, ethnicity, sex, and geography. Additionally, the entire analytic pipeline is documented and version-controlled so that the analysis can be rerun and extended as future cohorts complete the program — a summative design constructed to support ongoing monitoring. Throughout, I translated findings into visualizations and written summaries for program staff and system partners.

While H-SYNC was summative rather than ongoing performance monitoring, my broader training and experience map directly onto the monitoring, measurement, and continuous-improvement activities this role involves. My doctoral training is grounded in research methods, study design, and applied statistics, and my research career at UW (Department of Sociology, 2014–2026) has been sustained practice in study design under real-world constraints, measurement operationalization from imperfect instruments, and the iterative cycle of specifying, testing, and refining an analysis. I’m confident these skills transfer readily to developing performance measures, designing data-collection protocols, and building the dashboards and reporting that support continuous quality improvement.
